## Basic Critique Practice

In {{<link "/tutorials/4-critique">}}, I recommend students practice making critiques in a stylized form. I'll do some of that here. But, to focus on the basics of the design, let me give another nice example which makes some things a little clearer:

{{<rimage width="600" src="16183002.jpg" attr="From the David Rumsey Map Collection" attrlink="https://www.davidrumsey.com/luna/servlet/detail/RUMSEY~8~1~366453~90133713:Tableau-Comparatif-De-La-Hauteur-De#" caption="The artwork titled 'Tableau Comparatif De La Hauteur Des Montagnes de la longuer du Cours des Fleuves et de l'Elevation des Principales Chutes d'Eau Dans Les Cinq Parties Du Monde' was created by C.V. Monin, with contributions from Charles, Andre Basset, and Michel Perrot. It was published around 1840 by Basset Edr.">}}

Here is a critique in the stylized form:

> If the goal is to allow the viewer to compare one aspect of a complex object *(objective)*, stretching the rivers out so that their length goes along the vertical axis *(decision)* makes sense because it assigns the property that the viewer wants to compare to a primary visual variable and allows for side-by-side comparison *(principle)*.

What drew me to this map is how is is truly designed to support the comparative task: to help get a sense of the relative lengths of the rivers, they need to be put side by side in a way that the lengths can be compared. In this case, the author of the maps straightened the rivers so that the length of the river is a distance along the Y axis. This makes things easy to compare: we're using one of the most powerful visual chanels (position on a common axis) to encode what we care most about. 

Notice how the design is enabled by *task*: the task is to compare the lengths of the rivers; not to navigate the rivers, to understand where they go, compare their curviness, etc. The designer sacrificed what we don't care about to support what we do care about. To enable comparison, the designer created alignment.

That said: part of what makes this map works so well is that it is not hiding its "inaccuracies". I don't think anyone would mistake these rivers as being straight like this. On the contrary, the layout emphasizes that this is for comparison. 

Which connects to another thing that I love about this map: the rivers keep enough detail so you can recognize them, and see key points along them. I particularly like how the St. Lawrence shows how the great lakes are an ordered chain, and their relative lengths.

## Design for Comparison

By dividing the rivers by continent, it is easier to compare within a continent, but much more difficult to compare *between* continents. Actually, it might be impossible - I am not sure the scales are the same.

 ## History (of me and it)

 A few years ago, I saw this map:
 {{<rimage src="0152008.jpg" width="300" attr="From the David Rumsey Map Collection" attrlink="https://www.davidrumsey.com/luna/servlet/detail/RUMSEY~8~1~208918~5003926:Mountains-and-Rivers-in-the-World-" caption="Mountains and Rivers in the World, published in a 1869 atlas by Colton & Co. of New York. An example of my favorite visualization.">}}
 probably on this web site. And I loved it. I was doing the comparisons research project, and this map inspired me.

But, then, I started to see other ones like it. Other versions of the same design. Including a print on a colleagues office wall. It turns out that this isn't a one-off map: it was effectively an entire genre. Many different cartographers (or cartography publishers) created their own versions. In the 19th century, this was a popular design.

So, I was curious: where did this design come from? Which one was the first to use this design. So, I started to dig. And found that people had been producing maps with this same basic design since (at least) the early 19th century. There is even a paper that discusses its history and tries to figure out where it came from:

- Baptiste Hautdidier. 2015. The comparative tableau of mountains and rivers: emulation and reappraisal of a popular 19th-century visualization design. Environ Plan A 47, 6 (June 2015), 1265–1282. https://doi.org/10.1177/0308518X15594901

In the 19th century, maps and atlases (containing many maps) were a popular art form as an artistic and scholarly medium. People learned about the world from these maps, and studied them as ways of communicating the data. But they also served an artistic and entertainment role. And there was competition: many companies were making similar maps, so different creators needed to distinguish themselves, often through artistry. It was easy enough to copy (steal) the underlying information: companies needed other ways to distinguish themselves. (A fun, fictional take on this is the novel The Cartographers, which I recommend).

At one point, I wanted to get one for my wall. Either a real historic map, or a reproduction. And I started down the rabbit hole of seeing the range of variants of this design. As of the time I have written this, I still haven't picked one yet. I'd love to get a colorful hand-colored one...

## The Takeaways

Comparative maps are cool, and I love them. OK, maybe you don't care what I like: but I think you can learn from these examples.

The lesson: Design for comparison. To support comparison, find ways to achieve alignment - even if that requires making other concessions.
